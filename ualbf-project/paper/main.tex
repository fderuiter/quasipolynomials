\begin{abstract}
This paper presents the Unified Algebraic-Lattice Bipartition Framework (UALBF). 
We formalize key lemmas concerning the sum of divisors function ($\sigma$) and parity arguments using the Lean 4 theorem prover.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The problem of identifying the properties of quasiperfect numbers (QPNs) fundamentally relies on understanding the index structure of sums of divisors. When $N = 2m^2$ or $N = (2m+1)^2$, parity considerations yield powerful constraints.

A quasiperfect number (QPN) is a positive integer $N$ whose sum of divisors satisfies $\sigma(N) = 2N + 1$. While no such numbers have been found, their existence has been heavily constrained by decades of mathematical research. Cattaneo (1951) demonstrated that any QPN must be an odd perfect square \cite{cattaneo1951}. Subsequent computational and analytical efforts by Hagis and Cohen (1982) established that a QPN must be greater than $10^{35}$ and contain at least seven distinct prime factors \cite{hagis1982}. Despite these bounds, closing the search space remains computationally difficult due to combinatorial explosions in possible prime factorizations.

The Unified Algebraic-Lattice Bipartition Framework (UALBF) is motivated by the necessity to merge rigorous formal verification with high-performance computational search heuristics. Purely mathematical approaches struggle to eliminate vast swaths of candidate numbers without concrete enumeration, while purely computational searches are prone to unverified optimizations and integer overflow bugs that might silently skip a valid QPN. By formalizing our mathematical constraints in the Lean 4 theorem prover and leveraging a highly parallel Rust engine for the search, we achieve a verified, panic-free computational pipeline.

In this paper, we present the following core contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Formalized Parity and Divisibility Lemmas:} We provide mechanically verified proofs in Lean 4 that a QPN cannot be a double square, and establish the foundational $\sigma$ parity and exact valuation lemmas (\cref{sec:math_foundations}).
    \item \textbf{Exact Valuation Ray-Cast Strategy:} We introduce a ray-cast algorithm in Rust whose \emph{targeting step}---computing the unique residue class of $N_R$ modulo $\sigma(N_L)$ via a single modular inverse---runs in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time.  Candidates along the resulting arithmetic progression are then enumerated in $\mathcal{O}(z_{\max}/\sigma(N_L))$ time, with exact $p^{2e} \parallel N$ divisibility constraints used to aggressively prune each candidate (\cref{sec:computational_methodology}).
    \item \textbf{Extended Computational Bounds:} We successfully push the lower bounds for QPNs further, empirically validating our theoretical framework without encountering false positives (\cref{sec:results}).
\end{itemize}

\section{The Quasiperfect Number Problem}
\label{sec:qpn_problem}

For a positive integer $N$, the \emph{sum-of-divisors function} is
$\sigma(N) = \sum_{d \mid N} d$.
The \emph{abundancy index} of~$N$ is the ratio $\sigma(N)/N$.
A \emph{perfect number} satisfies $\sigma(N)=2N$; analogously, a \emph{quasiperfect number} (QPN) is a positive integer satisfying
\begin{equation}\label{eq:qpn-def}
  \sigma(N) \;=\; 2N + 1.
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Quasiperfect Number]\label{def:qpn}
A positive integer $N$ is \textbf{quasiperfect} if $N > 0$ and $\sigma(N) = 2N + 1$.
In our Lean~4 formalisation this is encoded directly:
\leaninline{def IsQuasiperfect (n : ℕ) : Prop := n > 0 ∧ sigma n = 2 * n + 1}.
\end{definition}

No quasiperfect number has ever been found.  Cattaneo~\cite{cattaneo1951}
showed that any QPN must be an odd perfect square, and Hagis and
Cohen~\cite{hagis1982} established that $N > 10^{35}$ with at least seven
distinct prime factors.  Because $2N+1$ is manifestly odd, one obtains
immediately that $\sigma(N)$ is odd for any QPN; the consequences of this
parity constraint are the starting point for the analysis below.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Properties of the Sum of Divisors Function}
\label{subsec:sigma_properties}

\paragraph{Multiplicativity.}
The sum-of-divisors function is \emph{multiplicative}: whenever
$\gcd(a,b)=1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigma-mult}
  \sigma(ab) \;=\; \sigma(a)\,\sigma(b).
\end{equation}
For a prime power $p^k$ the divisor sum telescopes to
$\sigma(p^{k})=\sum_{j=0}^{k}p^{j}=\frac{p^{k+1}-1}{p-1}$.
Both facts are available in Mathlib; in our codebase we invoke them through
\texttt{Nat.Coprime.sum\_divisors\_mul} and
\texttt{sum\_divisors\_prime\_pow}.

\paragraph{Parity characterisation (\texttt{odd\_sigma\_iff\_square\_or\_double\_square}).}
A classical result states:

\begin{theorem}[Parity of $\sigma$]\label{thm:parity-sigma}
For $N \ge 1$,
\[
  \sigma(N) \text{ is odd}
  \;\Longleftrightarrow\;
  N = k^{2} \;\text{ or }\; N = 2k^{2}
  \quad\text{for some } k \in \mathbb{N}.
\]
\end{theorem}

The proof proceeds in two stages, both formalised in \texttt{Basic.lean}.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Stage 1: factorisation bridge
(\texttt{odd\_sigma\_iff\_factorization}).}
Since $\sigma$ is multiplicative, $\sigma(N)=\prod_{p\mid N}\sigma(p^{v_p(N)})$.
Each factor $\sigma(p^{v})=\sum_{j=0}^{v}p^{j}$ has parity depending on
whether $p$ is even or odd:
\begin{itemize}
  \item If $p=2$, the geometric sum $1+2+\cdots+2^v$ is always odd.
  \item If $p$ is odd, the sum of $v+1$ odd terms is odd iff $v+1$ is odd,
        i.e.\ $v$ is even.
\end{itemize}
Hence $\sigma(N)$ is odd if and only if every \emph{odd} prime $p \mid N$ has
$v_p(N)$ even.  This equivalence is encoded as
\texttt{odd\_sigma\_iff\_factorization}.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Stage~2: structural decomposition
(\texttt{factorization\_even\_iff\_square\_or\_double\_square}).}
Write $N = 2^{e}\cdot u$ with $u$ odd.
If every odd prime factor of $N$ has even valuation, then every prime factor
of~$u$ (all odd) has even valuation, so $u$ is a perfect square.
When $e$ is even, $N = (2^{e/2})^2 u = (2^{e/2}\sqrt{u})^{2}$ is a perfect
square; when $e$ is odd, $N = 2 \cdot (2^{(e-1)/2}\sqrt{u})^{2}$ is twice a
perfect square.  The converse direction follows by direct computation of
valuations.

\smallskip\noindent
Combining these two stages yields \cref{thm:parity-sigma}.  As an immediate
corollary, since $\sigma(N)=2N+1$ is odd for any QPN, $N$ must be a perfect
square or a double square.

\paragraph{Elimination of double squares (\texttt{qpn\_not\_double\_square}).}
The double-square alternative is eliminated by a modulo~3 argument.
If $N = 2m^{2}$, write $m = 2^{e}u$ with $u$ odd, so
$N = 2^{2e+1} u^{2}$.  By multiplicativity,
$\sigma(N) = \sigma(2^{2e+1})\,\sigma(u^{2})$.
A short induction shows $\sigma(2^{2e+1})\equiv 0 \pmod{3}$ for all $e$,
hence $3 \mid \sigma(N)$.  Meanwhile the QPN equation gives
$\sigma(N) = 4m^{2}+1$, and one verifies by exhaustive case analysis on
$m \bmod 3$ that $4m^{2}+1 \not\equiv 0 \pmod{3}$---a contradiction.

\begin{theorem}[QPNs are Odd Perfect Squares \cite{cattaneo1951}]\label{thm:qpn-odd-sq}
Every quasiperfect number $N$ is an odd perfect square: $N$ is odd and
$N = m^{2}$ for some $m \in \mathbb{N}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (formalised as \texttt{qpn\_is\_odd\_square})]
By \cref{thm:parity-sigma}, $N$ is a square or a double square.  The
double-square case is excluded by the modulo~3 contradiction above.
If $N$ were even, it would have to be a double square
(\texttt{even\_qpn\_implies\_double\_square}), which is impossible; hence $N$
is odd.  If $N = m^{2}$ with $m$ even, a deeper parity obstruction via the
Legendre symbol (\texttt{square\_qpn\_parity\_obstruction}) produces a
contradiction by exhibiting a prime $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$ dividing
$2^{2e+1}-1 \mid u^{2}+1$, which forces $-1$ to be a quadratic residue mod
$q$---violating the first supplement to quadratic reciprocity.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Modulo~8 Obstructions and the Legendre Symbol}
\label{subsec:mod8_obstructions}

The parity analysis of \cref{subsec:sigma_properties} restricts $N$ to be an
odd perfect square $N = m^{2}$.  We now tighten the constraints on the prime
factors of $\sigma(N)$ using quadratic reciprocity.

\begin{theorem}[Universal Modulo-8 Obstruction]\label{thm:mod8}
Let $N$ be a quasiperfect number and let $q$ be an odd prime dividing
$\sigma(N)$.  Then $q \equiv 1$ or $3 \pmod{8}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (formalised as \texttt{legendre\_cattaneo\_obstruction})]
By \cref{thm:qpn-odd-sq}, $N = m^{2}$.  The QPN equation becomes
$\sigma(m^{2}) = 2m^{2}+1$.
If $q \mid \sigma(m^{2})$, then working in the field $\mathbb{Z}/q\mathbb{Z}$
we obtain:
\[
  2m^{2} + 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{q}
  \;\implies\;
  4m^{2} + 2 \equiv 0 \pmod{q}
  \;\implies\;
  (2m)^{2} \equiv -2 \pmod{q}.
\]
Thus $-2$ is a quadratic residue modulo $q$, giving
$\left(\frac{-2}{q}\right) = 1$.
By the evaluation of the Legendre symbol at $-2$ in terms of the
Dirichlet character $\chi_{8}'$ (Mathlib: \texttt{legendreSym.at\_neg\_two}),
we have $\left(\frac{-2}{q}\right)=\chi_{8}'(q)$.
Computing $\chi_{8}'(r)$ for $r \in \{0,\dots,7\}$ shows that
$\chi_{8}'(r)=1$ if and only if $r \in \{1,3\}$.
Therefore $q \bmod 8 \in \{1,3\}$.
\end{proof}

\cref{thm:mod8} is the cornerstone of the engine's pruning strategy: if any
prime factor of $\sigma(p^{2e})$ satisfies $q \equiv 5$ or $7 \pmod 8$, the
corresponding exact valuation $p^{2e} \parallel N$ is \emph{provably
impossible}, and the entire subtree of the DFS can be discarded.

\subsection{Exact Valuations and Divisibility}
\label{subsec:exact_valuations}

We write $p^{a} \parallel N$ (``$p^{a}$ exactly divides $N$'') to mean
$p^{a} \mid N$ but $p^{a+1} \nmid N$.
Since every QPN is an odd perfect square, the exponent of every prime in
its factorisation is even, so the relevant condition is always
$p^{2e} \parallel N$.

\begin{lemma}[Exact Valuation implies Sigma Divisibility]\label{lem:exact-val}
If $p$ is prime and $p^{2e} \parallel N$, then
$\sigma(p^{2e}) \mid \sigma(N)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (formalised as \texttt{exact\_val\_sigma\_dvd})]
Write $N = p^{2e} k$.  The exact-divisibility hypothesis implies
$p \nmid k$, whence $\gcd(p^{2e}, k) = 1$.  By multiplicativity
(\cref{eq:sigma-mult}), $\sigma(N) = \sigma(p^{2e})\,\sigma(k)$, and the
claim follows.
\end{proof}

Chaining \cref{lem:exact-val} with \cref{thm:mod8} yields the main soundness
guarantee for the Rust engine's sieve:

\begin{theorem}[Sieve Soundness]\label{thm:sieve-sound}
Let $N$ be a quasiperfect number, $p$ a prime, and $e \ge 0$.
If $\sigma(p^{2e})$ has an odd prime factor $q$ with
$q \equiv 5$ or $7 \pmod{8}$, then
$p^{2e} \nmid\!\mid N$ (i.e.\ this exact valuation cannot occur).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof (formalised as \texttt{rust\_sieve\_soundness})]
Assume for contradiction that $p^{2e} \parallel N$.
By \cref{lem:exact-val}, $\sigma(p^{2e}) \mid \sigma(N)$.
Since $q \mid \sigma(p^{2e})$, transitivity gives $q \mid \sigma(N)$.
\cref{thm:mod8} then forces $q \equiv 1$ or $3 \pmod{8}$,
contradicting $q \equiv 5$ or $7 \pmod{8}$.
\end{proof}

\cref{thm:sieve-sound} is the formal certificate that the Rust engine's
$\mathcal{O}(1)$ prime-factor check on $\sigma(p^{2e})$ never discards a
legitimate QPN candidate.  It justifies the aggressive pruning that makes
the exhaustive search computationally tractable.

\subsection{The Prasad-Sunitha Bound for Coprime Prefixes}
\label{subsec:prasad_sunitha}

While Hagis and Cohen established that $\omega(N) \ge 7$ for any quasiperfect number \cite{hagis1982}, this bound can be significantly tightened when certain small prime factors are known to be absent. In particular, we formalise the Prasad-Sunitha bound \cite{prasad_sunitha} for quasiperfect numbers coprime to 15.

\begin{theorem}[Prasad-Sunitha Bound]\label{thm:prasad-sunitha}
Let $N$ be a quasiperfect number such that $\gcd(N, 15) = 1$. Then $N$ has at least 15 distinct prime factors ($\omega(N) \ge 15$).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (formally verified as \texttt{qpn\_coprime\_15\_omega\_15})]
We proceed by contradiction. Assume $\omega(N) \le 14$. From the definition of a QPN (\cref{def:qpn}), $\sigma(N) = 2N + 1 > 2N$. Using the multiplicativity of the sum-of-divisors function and bounding the contribution for prime powers, we obtain the cross-multiplied abundancy bound:
\[
  2 \prod_{p \mid N} (p - 1) < \prod_{p \mid N} p.
\]
Because $\gcd(N, 15) = 1$ and every QPN is an odd square (\cref{thm:qpn-odd-sq}), $N$ cannot be divisible by 2, 3, or 5. Thus, every prime factor of $N$ satisfies $p \ge 7$ (\texttt{qpn\_coprime\_15\_primes\_ge\_7}). 

The product $\prod \frac{p}{p-1}$ is strictly anti-monotonic with respect to prime substitution. Evaluating this product over the smallest 14 primes $p \ge 7$ (the constants $c_i \in \{7, 11, 13, \dots, 59\}$), we find that their combined abundancy contribution is strictly less than 2. This implies $\prod_{p \mid N} p \le 2 \prod_{p \mid N} (p - 1)$ (\texttt{finset\_euler\_bound}), which contradicts our strict inequality derived from the QPN definition. Thus, we must have $\omega(N) \ge 15$.
\end{proof}

This theoretical bound is computationally critical. When the Rust engine's depth-first search branch asserts that 3 and 5 are not factors of $N$, the engine dynamically requires a minimum of 15 prime factors for the active prefix, drastically pruning the search space.

\section{The Unified Algebraic-Lattice Bipartition Framework}
\label{sec:ualbf}

By \cref{thm:qpn-odd-sq}, every QPN is an odd perfect square
$N = \prod_{i} p_i^{2e_i}$
with each $p_i$ an odd prime.  The key idea of the UALBF is to
\emph{bipartition} this factorisation into a \emph{prefix} $N_L$ and a
\emph{suffix} $N_R$, chosen so that all arithmetic constraints induced by the
QPN equation decompose cleanly across the split.  The prefix is constructed
incrementally by the Rust engine's depth-first search, while the suffix's
residue class modulo $\sigma(N_L)$ is determined by a constant-time
modular inverse; candidates in that class are then enumerated and sieved.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Bipartitioning the Search Space}
\label{subsec:bipartition}

\begin{definition}[QPN Bipartition]\label{def:bipartition}
A \textbf{QPN bipartition} of a quasiperfect number $N$ is a triple
$(N, N_L, N_R)$ satisfying:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $N_L > 0$, $N_R > 0$;
  \item $N = N_L \cdot N_R$;
  \item $\gcd(N_L, N_R) = 1$.
\end{enumerate}
In our formalisation this is captured by the Lean structure
\texttt{QpnBipartition} in \texttt{UALBF/Bipartition.lean}.
\end{definition}

Since $N$ is square-free in the radical sense (all exponents are even but
each prime appears at most once in the bipartition assignment), any partition
of the prime set of $N$ into two disjoint subsets induces a valid bipartition.
Concretely, the Rust engine's DFS assigns the first $k$ primes to $N_L$ and
all remaining primes to $N_R$; coprimality is automatic because the prime
sets are disjoint.

\paragraph{Multiplicativity over the bipartition (\texttt{sigma\_mul}).}
Because $\gcd(N_L, N_R) = 1$, the sum-of-divisors function factors:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigma-bipartition}
  \sigma(N) \;=\; \sigma(N_L)\,\sigma(N_R).
\end{equation}
Substituting the QPN equation $\sigma(N) = 2N + 1 = 2 N_L N_R + 1$ yields
the \emph{fundamental bipartition identity}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bipartition-identity}
  \sigma(N_L)\,\sigma(N_R) \;=\; 2\,N_L\,N_R + 1.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Coprimality of prefix and its divisor sum
(\texttt{prefix\_sigma\_coprime}).}
The identity~\eqref{eq:bipartition-identity} has an important structural
consequence:

\begin{theorem}[Prefix--Sigma Coprimality]\label{thm:prefix-coprime}
For any QPN bipartition $(N, N_L, N_R)$,
\[
  \gcd\bigl(N_L,\;\sigma(N_L)\bigr) \;=\; 1.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof (formalised as \texttt{prefix\_sigma\_coprime})]
Let $d = \gcd(N_L, \sigma(N_L))$.  Then $d \mid N_L$ and
$d \mid \sigma(N_L)$.  From $d \mid \sigma(N_L)$ and
$\sigma(N_L) \mid \sigma(N_L)\,\sigma(N_R)$, we obtain
$d \mid \sigma(N_L)\,\sigma(N_R)$.
From $d \mid N_L$ and $N_L \mid N_L N_R$, we obtain
$d \mid 2 N_L N_R$.
By~\eqref{eq:bipartition-identity} both sides are equal, so
\[
  d \mid \sigma(N_L)\,\sigma(N_R)
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  d \mid 2\,N_L\,N_R.
\]
Subtracting: $d \mid (\sigma(N_L)\,\sigma(N_R) - 2\,N_L\,N_R) = 1$.
Hence $d = 1$.
\end{proof}

\cref{thm:prefix-coprime} is operationally critical: it guarantees that the
modular inverse of $2 N_L$ modulo $\sigma(N_L)$ always exists, so the Rust
engine's ray-cast subroutine can never encounter a division-by-zero panic.

\paragraph{The AMBS suffix target (\texttt{ambs\_suffix\_target}).}
Casting~\eqref{eq:bipartition-identity} into the ring
$\mathbb{Z}/\sigma(N_L)\mathbb{Z}$, the left-hand side vanishes (since
$\sigma(N_L) \equiv 0$), giving
\[
  0 \;=\; 2\,N_L\,N_R + 1
  \qquad\text{in } \mathbb{Z}/\sigma(N_L)\mathbb{Z}.
\]
Rearranging:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ambs-target}
  N_R \cdot (2\,N_L) \;\equiv\; -1 \pmod{\sigma(N_L)}.
\end{equation}
Because $\gcd(2 N_L, \sigma(N_L)) = 1$ (by \cref{thm:prefix-coprime} and
the fact that $\sigma(N_L)$ is odd for a QPN), the value of $N_R$ modulo
$\sigma(N_L)$ is uniquely determined.  This is the \emph{Algebraic-Modular
Bipartition Sieve} (AMBS) target: given a prefix $N_L$, the engine computes
the unique residue class of $N_R$ in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time via a single
modular inverse, then enumerates all representatives
$z \equiv r \pmod{\sigma(N_L)}$ in the interval $[z_{\min},\, z_{\max}]$
in $\mathcal{O}(z_{\max}/\sigma(N_L))$ time, applying the mod-8 sieve
and coprimality checks to each candidate.

\subsection{Parity Contradictions for Double Squares}
\label{subsec:double_squares}

The restriction to odd perfect squares established in \cref{thm:qpn-odd-sq}
rests on two independent contradictions.  The modulo-3 argument
(\texttt{qpn\_not\_double\_square}, cf.\ \cref{subsec:sigma_properties})
eliminates the double-square form $N = 2m^2$ outright.  Here we detail the
deeper Legendre-symbol obstruction that eliminates even perfect squares.

\begin{theorem}[Even Square QPN Obstruction]\label{thm:even-sq-obstruction}
If $N = m^2$ is a QPN and $m$ is even, then a contradiction arises.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (formalised as
\texttt{square\_qpn\_parity\_obstruction})]
Write $m = 2^e u$ with $u$ odd and $e \ge 1$, so
$N = 2^{2e}\,u^2$.  The QPN equation and multiplicativity of $\sigma$ give
\[
  (2^{2e+1}-1)\,\sigma(u^2) \;=\; 2^{2e+1}\,u^2 + 1.
\]
Set $M = 2^{2e+1}-1$.  Rewriting the right-hand side as
$(M+1)\,u^2 + 1 = M\,u^2 + u^2 + 1$, we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M-divides}
  M \mid (u^2 + 1).
\end{equation}
Since $2e+1 \ge 3$, we have $M = 2^{2e+1}-1 \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ (by
\texttt{two\_pow\_sub\_one\_mod\_four}).  A classical lemma
(\texttt{mod\_four\_eq\_three\_has\_prime\_factor}) then guarantees a prime
$q \mid M$ with $q \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$.

By~\eqref{eq:M-divides} and transitivity, $q \mid u^2 + 1$, so
$u^2 \equiv -1 \pmod{q}$.
But $-1$ is a quadratic residue modulo a prime $q$ if and only if
$q \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ (the first supplement to quadratic reciprocity;
Mathlib: \texttt{ZMod.exists\_sq\_eq\_neg\_one\_iff}).  Since
$q \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, this is impossible.
\end{proof}

Combined with the modulo-3 elimination of $N = 2m^2$,
\cref{thm:even-sq-obstruction} closes every non-odd-square alternative.
The UALBF therefore searches exclusively over factorisations of the form
$N = \prod_i p_i^{2e_i}$ with each $p_i$ odd, with the bipartition
machinery of this section reducing each candidate to a single modular
equation~\eqref{eq:ambs-target}.

\section{Computational Methodology}
\label{sec:computational_methodology}

\subsection{Depth-First Search for Prefix Construction}
\label{subsec:dfs_prefix}
Our Rust engine constructs prefixes using a Depth-First Search (DFS) over prime factorizations, applying early pruning based on exact valuations $\nu_p(N)$. The formalization of $\nu_p(N)$ and the condition that $\sigma(p^{2e}) \mid \sigma(N)$ grounds the computational search in verified mathematics \cite{moura2021,mathlib2020}.

\subsection{Orchestration and Ray-Casting}
\label{subsec:ray_casting}
The ray-cast strategy serves as the core enumeration engine of the UALBF. Given a prefix $N_L$, the algorithm determines the unique residue class of the suffix $N_R$ modulo $\sigma(N_L)$ in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time. This targeting step is achieved via a fast 128-bit modular inverse function, \texttt{mod\_inverse\_128}, which is validated through a Lean Foreign Function Interface (FFI) to guarantee mathematical correctness. Once the arithmetic progression is established, candidates for $N_R$ are enumerated in $\mathcal{O}(z_{\max}/\sigma(N_L))$ time. Each candidate is then subjected to rigorous exact sieving, where we apply $\mathcal{O}(1)$ exact valuation divisibility checks (\cref{lem:exact-val}) to aggressively prune invalid suffixes before any expensive factorization attempts.

\subsection{Z3 CDCL Pruner}
\label{subsec:z3_cdcl_pruner}
The UALBF engine integrates a Z3-based Conflict-Driven Clause Learning (CDCL) pruner to aggressively eliminate unviable search branches. By encoding the current partial factorisation and its resulting abundancy constraints into SMT formulas, the Z3 solver can detect Zsigmondy traps and target abundance starvation using dependency learning. This prevents the depth-first search from exploring deeply into subtrees that are mathematically guaranteed to fail.

\subsection{LLL Lattice Diophantine Pruning}
\label{subsec:lll_lattice_pruning}
To shape the top of the search tree early (specifically depths 3 to 6), the engine employs Lenstra–Lenstra–Lovász (LLL) lattice basis reduction. Using arbitrary-precision arithmetic provided by the \texttt{rug::Integer} crate, the LLL algorithm identifies whether the remaining required multiplicands can plausibly satisfy the tight Diophantine constraints of the QPN equation (\cref{eq:qpn-def}). If no shortest vector solving the relaxed subset-sum problem exists within the allowable tolerance, the entire prefix is pruned before expensive deeper enumeration occurs.

\subsection{Lock-Free Concurrency \& Telemetry}
\label{subsec:concurrency_telemetry}
The computational pipeline utilizes the Rust Rayon crate for dynamic, work-stealing concurrency. To maximize parallel throughput and avoid the thundering-herd problem, thread synchronization is managed strictly via lock-free atomic states and read-write locks, minimizing blocking. This lock-free architecture not only ensures CPU cores remain fully saturated but also drives real-time telemetry, allowing external monitoring tools to track the active primes and search progress dynamically without introducing observational overhead.

\subsection{Dynamic Minimum Factors}
\label{subsec:dynamic_minimum_factors}
Leveraging our formalized Prasad-Sunitha bound (\cref{thm:prasad-sunitha}), the computational search floor dynamically shifts during deep exploration. When the depth-first search branch definitively excludes 3 and 5 as prime factors of a candidate $N$, the engine's dynamic constraints automatically elevate the minimum required distinct prime factors from the baseline of 7 up to 15. This dynamic adjustment drastically accelerates the search by instantly rejecting branches that cannot possibly reach the required abundancy index.
